# Annotated Reading List — Pedagogical Foundations of Socrates

This list organises the primary and secondary literature underpinning Socrates' design. Each entry includes a one-sentence annotation explaining what it contributes *beyond* what the README covers. Organised by framework in the order they appear in the skill's protocol.

---

## 1. The Socratic Method

### Primary Sources

**Plato. (c. 380 BCE / trans. Jowett, B., 1892). *Meno*. In *The dialogues of Plato* (Vol. 1). Oxford University Press.**
The locus classicus for both the elenctic method and the theory of recollection (*anamnesis*) — the metaphysical claim that learning is remembering, which grounds the skill's assumption that the learner's own reasoning contains latent insight worth surfacing.

**Plato. (c. 369 BCE / trans. Jowett, B., 1892). *Theaetetus*. In *The dialogues of Plato* (Vol. 4). Oxford University Press.**
Introduces the midwifery metaphor (*maieutics*) explicitly: Socrates describes himself as helping others give birth to ideas they already carry. The README covers this; the dialogue itself clarifies that maieutics applies only when there is genuine knowledge to draw out — Socrates turns people away when they have nothing latent.

### Secondary Sources

**Vlastos, G. (1983). The Socratic elenchus. *Oxford Studies in Ancient Philosophy*, *1*, 27–58.**
Distinguishes "standard elenchus" (refutation of a specific thesis through the interlocutor's own admissions) from the broader Platonic dialectic; argues that the elenchus is not merely negative but has a positive epistemic function — a key clarification for understanding *why* the reflection protocol must precede gap revelation. Beyond the README: this is the paper that establishes the elenchus as truth-tracking rather than merely persuasive.

**Robinson, R. (1953). *Plato's earlier dialectic* (2nd ed.). Oxford University Press.**
A foundational analytic study of how the elenchus operates across the early dialogues, including its failure modes (when interlocutors are unwilling to follow the argument). Beyond the README: Robinson identifies that the elenchus requires the interlocutor's genuine assent at each step — a structural reason why Socrates (the skill) must not allow skipping or reordering of phases.

## 2. Vygotsky's Zone of Proximal Development

### Primary Source

**Vygotsky, L. S. (1978). *Mind in society: The development of higher psychological processes* (M. Cole, V. John-Steiner, S. Scribner, & E. Souberman, Eds.). Harvard University Press.**
Chapter 6 contains the canonical ZPD definition and the argument that instruction must precede development — not follow it — to be effective. The README cites p. 86; beyond that, Chapter 6 also establishes that collaborative problem-solving with a more capable partner is mechanistically distinct from solo performance, which is why the skill's case study explicitly withdraws the partner during the attempt.

### Secondary Sources

**Wood, D., Bruner, J. S., & Ross, G. (1976). The role of tutoring in problem solving. *Journal of Child Psychology and Psychiatry*, *17*(2), 89–100. https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1469-7610.1976.tb00381.x**
Introduces "scaffolding" as the operational mechanism through which ZPD is traversed — the tutor does what the learner cannot yet do, then withdraws as competence grows. Beyond the README: the paper establishes *contingency* as the defining feature of effective scaffolding (support is adjusted in real time to the learner's current state), which is exactly what the diagnostic enables.

**Wertsch, J. V. (1985). *Vygotsky and the social formation of mind*. Harvard University Press.**
A rigorous close reading of Vygotsky clarifying that ZPD is a dynamic relationship, not a fixed measurement — it shifts as instruction proceeds. Beyond the README: this reframes the skill's formative checkpoints as ZPD re-mappings, not just progress tests; the boundary moves after each successful phase.

## 3. Bloom's Taxonomy — Original and Revised

### Primary Sources

**Bloom, B. S., Engelhart, M. D., Furst, E. J., Hill, W. H., & Krathwohl, D. R. (1956). *Taxonomy of educational objectives: The classification of educational goals. Handbook I: Cognitive domain*. David McKay Company.**
The original six-category taxonomy (Knowledge, Comprehension, Application, Analysis, Synthesis, Evaluation) with the philosophical argument that educational objectives must be stated in terms of student behaviour, not subject matter. Beyond the README: the original taxonomy uses nouns (Knowledge, Comprehension); understanding why Anderson & Krathwohl switched to verbs requires reading this first.

**Anderson, L. W., & Krathwohl, D. R. (Eds.). (2001). *A taxonomy for learning, teaching, and assessing: A revision of Bloom's educational objectives*. Longman.**
The revision that moves to verbs (Remember, Understand, Apply, Analyse, Evaluate, Create), separates the knowledge dimension from the cognitive process dimension, and adds a two-dimensional table. The README's Bloom table is drawn from this; beyond it, the knowledge dimension (factual, conceptual, procedural, metacognitive) is a useful secondary axis for calibrating case study difficulty.

### Secondary Sources

**Krathwohl, D. R. (2002). A revision of Bloom's taxonomy: An overview. *Theory Into Practice*, *41*(4), 212–218. https://doi.org/10.1207/s15430421tip4104_2**
The lead author of the revision explains the rationale for each change, particularly the verb/noun shift and the promotion of "Create" above "Evaluate." Beyond the README: Krathwohl explains that "Synthesis" was renamed "Create" and moved to the apex because producing novel work is cognitively harder than evaluating existing work — directly relevant to why the skill's curriculum treats Create as the terminal objective.

**Hattie, J., & Donoghue, G. (2016). Learning strategies: A synthesis and conceptual model. *npj Science of Learning*, *1*, Article 16013. https://doi.org/10.1038/npjscilearn.2016.13**
Situates Bloom-level tasks within a model of surface, deep, and transfer learning; demonstrates empirically that strategies effective at one level are often ineffective at another. Beyond the README: this explains *why* Socrates cannot simply dump all content at once — the learner's current Bloom level determines which learning strategies will work, not just which content to cover.

## 4. Flavell's Metacognitive Framework

### Primary Source

**Flavell, J. H. (1979). Metacognition and cognitive monitoring: A new area of cognitive–developmental inquiry. *American Psychologist*, *34*(10), 906–911. https://doi.org/10.1037/0003-066X.34.10.906**
The paper that established metacognition as a research programme and introduced the tripartite model (metacognitive knowledge, regulation, experience). Beyond the README: Flavell distinguishes *person variables* (what you know about your own cognition), *task variables* (what you know about how task features affect difficulty), and *strategy variables* (what you know about which strategies work) — all three are probed across the five diagnostic questions.

### Secondary Sources

**Dunning, D., Johnson, K., Ehrlinger, J., & Kruger, J. (2003). Why people fail to recognize their own incompetence. *Current Directions in Psychological Science*, *12*(3), 83–87. https://doi.org/10.1111/1467-8721.01235**
Empirical follow-up to the Kruger & Dunning (1999) studies demonstrating that metacognitive inaccuracy is not random — it is systematically tied to competence level, with the least competent showing the greatest overconfidence. Beyond the README: this paper establishes *why* the Unconsciously Incompetent quadrant is the most dangerous state — the learner lacks the metacognitive resources to recognise their own incompetence, making external elicitation through the case study structurally necessary.

**Brown, A. L. (1987). Metacognition, executive control, self-regulation, and other more mysterious mechanisms. In F. E. Weinert & R. H. Kluwe (Eds.), *Metacognition, motivation and understanding* (pp. 65–116). Lawrence Erlbaum.**
Distinguishes metacognitive *monitoring* (knowing what you know) from metacognitive *control* (doing something about it); argues that monitoring without control is insufficient for learning improvement. Beyond the README: this distinction maps directly onto the gap between Phase 3 Reflection Question 1 (eliciting monitoring) and Reflection Question 2 (eliciting control — what would you do differently), explaining why both questions are necessary rather than either alone.

## 5. Black & Wiliam's Formative Assessment

### Primary Sources

**Black, P., & Wiliam, D. (1998a). Assessment and classroom learning. *Assessment in Education: Principles, Policy & Practice*, *5*(1), 7–74. https://doi.org/10.1080/0969595980050102**
The meta-analysis of over 250 studies establishing formative assessment as the highest-leverage intervention in education (effect sizes 0.4–0.7 SD). Beyond the README: the paper distinguishes feedback that tells learners *what to do next* from feedback that tells them *how well they did* — only the former produces the effect size gains, which is why Socrates' Phase 3 observations are always forward-looking.

**Black, P., & Wiliam, D. (1998b). Inside the black box: Raising standards through classroom assessment. *Phi Delta Kappan*, *80*(2), 139–148.**
The practitioner-facing summary identifying five strategies. The README maps these strategies to skill phases; beyond that, the article emphasises that most assessment in practice is summative disguised as formative — teachers ask questions and then answer them themselves, denying students the time and cognitive engagement needed for genuine self-assessment.

### Secondary Sources

**Hattie, J. (2008). *Visible learning: A synthesis of over 800 meta-analyses relating to achievement*. Routledge.**
Places formative feedback (d = 0.73) among the highest-effect interventions across 800+ meta-analyses; distinguishes feedback *about the task*, feedback *about the process*, feedback *about self-regulation*, and feedback *about the self* (the last being the least effective). Beyond the README: Hattie's framework clarifies that Socrates' feedback must operate at the process and self-regulation levels — "you missed X" (task-level) is less effective than "here's the reasoning step that would have surfaced X" (process-level).

**Wiliam, D. (2011). *Embedded formative assessment*. Solution Tree Press.**
A practitioner manual translating the Black & Wiliam research into specific classroom strategies, with evidence on which implementations produce the largest gains. Beyond the README: Wiliam distinguishes five "key strategies" implementation patterns and notes that "activating students as owners of their own learning" is the hardest to implement and the most frequently skipped — which is precisely why Socrates makes Phase 3 Reflection Question 2 non-optional and non-skippable.

## Cross-Framework Reading

For readers who want to understand how these frameworks interact:

**Hattie, J., & Timperley, H. (2007). The power of feedback. *Review of Educational Research*, *77*(1), 81–112. https://doi.org/10.3102/003465430298487**
Synthesises Vygotsky's ZPD, Bloom's levels, and formative feedback into a unified model of effective instructional feedback. The paper's "feed forward" concept — feedback oriented toward the next learning step — is the theoretical basis for why Socrates' curriculum is generated *after* the quadrant map, not before it.
